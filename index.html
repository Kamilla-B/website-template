History

The Buryats are one of the most numerous indigenous peoples in Siberia, primarily residing in the Republic of Buryatia, the Ust-Orda Buryat Okrug, and various districts within the Irkutsk Region. They also live in northern Mongolia and northeastern China. As of the 2010 census, 461,400 Buryats lived in Russia. The Buryats emerged from the unification of diverse ethnic groups in the Baikal region, predominantly Mongolian tribal groups, supplemented by local Tungus and Turkic tribes. The establishment of the Russian-Chinese border in 1727 marked the consolidation of the Buryat identity. The Buryat language, part of the Northern Mongolian group, is an official language in the Republic of Buryatia and is used in education and media. Historically, cattle breeding was the primary economic activity, with hunting and fishing playing lesser roles. The transition to a sedentary lifestyle began in the 17th century and was nearly complete by the late 19th century, with Buryats increasingly engaging in agriculture and later integrating into diversified industries. The Buryats are divided into four main tribes and several territorial groups, each with its own unique heritage and cultural practices.
